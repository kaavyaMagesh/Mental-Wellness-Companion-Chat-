# Grief Support: Supporting a Grieving Friend or Family Member

## Overview
Supporting someone who is grieving works best when it is practical, patient, and consistent. Many people want to help but do not know what to say or do. The most useful support is often simple: being present, listening without judgment, and offering concrete help. Grief can be isolating, so steady kindness can make a real difference.

## Key Concepts / Symptoms
A grieving person may be quiet, distracted, emotional, or even appear unchanged on the outside. Their needs may shift over time, and they may not always ask for help directly. They may also repeat stories, forget plans, or need more patience than usual. These reactions are normal and should not be interpreted as withdrawal or lack of gratitude.

## Coping Strategies
Listen more than you speak, and avoid trying to fix the grief. Offer specific help such as meals, rides, errands, childcare, or help with paperwork. Use simple phrases like “I’m here for you” or “You do not have to go through this alone.” Check in again after the first few days, because support often drops off too soon.

## When to Seek Support
Encourage professional help if the person seems unable to function, becomes severely withdrawn, or talks about hopelessness or self-harm. If they are open to it, a counselor or support group may provide extra stability and care.
